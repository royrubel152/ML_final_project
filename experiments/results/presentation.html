Project Introduction

What Is This & Who Is It For?

The Problem

Before This Project

  • MBA students send dozens of repetitive emails to the secretariat daily
  • Questions like "which courses are mandatory?" answered manually, one by one
  • Secretariat staff spend hours on routine Q&A instead of complex cases
  • Students wait 24–48 hours for simple factual answers
  • Information scattered across PDF regulations, website, Shnaton — hard to navigate

Our Solution

An AI chatbot trained on all official HUJI MBA sources that answers student questions instantly, in Hebrew, 24/7 — with citations to the exact regulation or course page.

Who Uses It
🎓

MBA Students (Primary)

Ask questions about course requirements, exemptions, registration deadlines, specialization tracks — and get an immediate, sourced Hebrew answer.

🏛️

Secretariat Staff (Beneficiary)

Handles repetitive Q&A automatically. Staff focus on complex, personal cases that genuinely need human judgment.

📋

Program Administration

Add new Q&A pairs via a simple CSV — no code needed. The bot auto-updates on restart. Useful for policy changes, new deadlines, special announcements.

The core problem: MBA students waste time searching across PDFs and websites, and the secretariat wastes time answering the same questions repeatedly. Our bot closes that gap — it knows everything in the official sources and answers instantly in Hebrew. The secretariat can also inject new Q&A pairs via CSV without touching any code, making it a living system that stays up to date.

🔒

Production Hardening

Rate limiting (15 req/min per IP), API key validation at startup, 24h content cache, file-based session persistence (2h TTL), citation verifier that flags unverified quotes.

The Shnaton integration is what makes course data accurate and live. The CSV-based FAQ system is what makes the secretariat self-sufficient — they can update the bot's knowledge without any developer involvement.

Design Decisions

Key Decisions We Made

1

Semantic Chunking over Fixed-Size

Split on section headers (שנה X — חובה/בחירה) to keep course context intact. Fixed-size would break mid-course and lose the year/track label.

2

200-char Context Prefix on Continuation Chunks

Every chunk beyond the first in a section gets the header prepended, so retrieval always knows what course/year it belongs to.

3

Hebrew Morphological Prefix Expansion

Hebrew attaches prepositions to words (בקורס = "in the course"). We expand every query token with ב, ל, ה, ש, ו, מ, כ variants to avoid missing exact matches.

4

FAQ Integration via CSV

Secretary Q&A pairs embedded directly as "שאלה: ... תשובה: ..." chunks. Adding new rows and restarting auto-rebuilds the index via MD5 hash.

5

Deterministic Evaluation Only

No LLM-as-judge to avoid cost and non-determinism. URL-level Recall@k and MRR are reproducible and free — good enough to rank retrieval pipelines objectively.

6

Entity Boost for Course Codes

If a query contains a 5-digit course code, matching chunks get a ×1.3 RRF score boost. Course codes are precise identifiers — we surface them first.

אף אחת מההחלטות האלו אינה מובנת מאליה — כל אחת פותרת בעיה אמיתית ב-NLP עברי. המשפיעה ביותר: ההדבקה העברית (תחיליות ב/ל/ה) גורמת לכך שאותה מילה מופיעה ב-7 צורות שונות ומעלה, ומודלים דחוסים מפספסים את ההתאמות. תחילית ההקשר בקטעי המשך הייתה קריטית — בלעדיה, קטע על "מימון — שנה ב' בחירה" לא הכיל תווית שנה או מסלול.

Why This is the Baseline

  • Simplest possible approach — no special chunking
  • 500-char fixed-size chunks, 50-char overlap
  • No Hebrew query expansion, no BM25, no reranking
  • MiniLM: tiny model (~400 MB), fast on CPU

Limitations

  • Chunks break mid-sentence, mid-regulation
  • No section header context — chunk loses its topic label
  • 384-dim embeddings weaker than E5-large (1024) or Gemini (3072)
  • No lexical fallback for exact Hebrew term matching

Results — ⚠️ Read Carefully

  • R@10: 83% · MRR: 0.587
  • Numbers are inflated — metric artifact
  • 924 chunks all share coarse URLs (whole PDF = 1 URL)
  • Any chunk from the right page = "hit", even wrong paragraph
  • LLM receives wrong text → wrong answer despite "hit"

Why the metric is misleading here: URL-level Recall@k rewards retrieving any chunk from the right source page, not the right content. P1 has 924 large chunks with coarse URL granularity — statistically one lands in top-10 even if irrelevant. P2/P3/P4 use semantic chunks with the same URL set, making them directly comparable to each other but not to P1.

P1 is the simplest approach — but its high numbers are misleading. The metric counts a "hit" if any retrieved chunk shares the right URL, even if it's the wrong paragraph. P1 has 924 chunks all mapped to broad source URLs (the whole PDF = one URL), so statistically one chunk from the right source lands in top-10. But that chunk may say the wrong thing entirely — Gemini answers incorrectly. This is why we can't compare P1 directly to P2/P3/P4.

Why Hybrid?

  • BM25 catches exact Hebrew terms dense models miss
  • Dense catches semantic meaning BM25 misses
  • RRF is robust — no score calibration needed
  • Free: no API, runs fully local

E5-Large Details

  • intfloat/multilingual-e5-large
  • MTEB SOTA on multilingual retrieval
  • Requires "passage: " / "query: " instruction prefix
  • ~1.1 GB RAM, ~18 min to embed 658 chunks on CPU

Results

  • R@5: 31% (below P2 at low k)
  • R@10: 50%
  • R@50: 87% (beats P2!)
  • MRR: 0.133
  • Best at: accelerated_tracks (MRR 0.382)
P3 combines a dense E5-large model with a BM25 sparse index. BM25 is classic keyword search — it catches exact Hebrew terms the neural model misses, especially 5-digit course codes. RRF fusion combines both rankings without needing to tune score thresholds. Interestingly, P3 beats Gemini at large k (k≥20), but lags behind at low k — the reranker in P4 fixes that.

Why Parent-Child?

  • Children (250 chars) are precise → match exact query terms
  • Parents (900 chars) have full section context → better for LLM
  • Best of both: precise matching + rich context
  • 658 parents → 2,341 children

Why Cross-Encoder Reranker?

  • Bi-encoders (P2/P3) encode query & chunk independently
  • Cross-encoder attends across both texts jointly
  • Much higher precision — but only feasible on top-N candidates
  • Model: BAAI/bge-reranker-base (~280 MB)

Results (k_children=150)

  • R@3: 55% (vs 18% prod)
  • R@5: 69% (vs 40% prod)
  • R@10: 81% (vs 60% prod)
  • MRR: 0.296 (vs 0.156 prod)
  • Best pipeline by every metric at k ≤ 10
The key insight: small chunks (250 chars) are better for matching, but large chunks (900 chars) are better for answering. P4 uses both — it retrieves small children for precision, maps them back to their large parent for context, then uses a cross-encoder that reads the full query and parent together to re-score. This "attending across both texts" is what makes cross-encoders much more accurate than bi-encoders.

Conclusions & Next Steps

What We Learned & Where We Go Next

🏆 P4 is the Winner

Among semantic pipelines, P4 dominates at all k ≤ 10. R@10: 81% vs 60% (Gemini). MRR nearly doubles (0.156 → 0.296). P1's higher raw numbers are a URL-granularity artifact — right page, not right answer.

⚠️ Metrics Can Deceive

P1 (MiniLM + fixed chunks) scores R@10=83% but its 924 coarse chunks game the URL-level metric. Chatbot quality requires the LLM to receive the right text, not just a chunk from the right source page.

🔍 Hard Categories Remain

course_planning and registration_rules are weak across all semantic pipelines — multi-hop questions spanning several pages. No retrieval trick fixes this without better data coverage.

Next: Integrate P4 into Production

Replace the current Gemini-only pipeline in rag.py with P4's parent-child + reranker approach. Requires E5-large + bge-reranker-base (~1.4 GB total).

Next: End-to-End API Validation

Start the MBA server and evaluate full answer quality via localhost:8000 — not just URL retrieval but whether the LLM generates correct Hebrew answers.

Next: Expand Ground Truth

100 questions is a good start. Secretary can continuously add Q&A pairs to the CSV — they auto-embed on server restart with no code changes needed.

Key takeaway: naive chunking (P1) can fool URL-level metrics but fails to deliver the right content to the LLM. Dense embeddings alone (P2) hit a ceiling at 60%. The combination of BM25 + cross-encoder reranking on semantic chunks (P4) is what makes the real jump to 81%. Hard categories like course_planning remain weak because those questions span multiple pages — a problem for the LLM layer, not just retrieval.

Final Decision

We Choose: Pipeline P4 & K = 10

Why P4?
1

Best Recall Among Semantic Pipelines

R@10 = 81% vs 60% (Gemini) vs 50% (E5+BM25). P1 has higher raw numbers but retrieves the wrong text — P4 retrieves the correct paragraph. That is what the LLM needs to answer correctly.

2

MRR Nearly Doubles Production

MRR 0.296 vs 0.156 (Gemini). The correct source ranks on average nearly twice as high in the result list.

3

Small-to-Big = Precision + Context

Children (250 chars) match precise terms. Parents (900 chars) give the LLM full section context. Best of both worlds with k_children=150 tuned by sweep.

4

Free After Setup

No API cost per query. E5-large + bge-reranker run locally. Only the LLM (Gemini 3.0-Flash) costs money.

The Elbow Rule

K=3 → K=10 gains +26 percentage points in recall. After K=10 the cross-encoder has exhausted its k_parents=20 candidate pool — adding more K costs the LLM context with no extra retrieval benefit. K=10 is the optimal cost/quality trade-off.

Result: Updated TOP_K = 10 in rag.py. k_children=150 set as P4 default after sweep. Production chatbot now retrieves 10 rich parent chunks per query.

Our two decisions: Pipeline P4 (parent-child + cross-encoder, tuned with k_children=150) and K=10. P1 looks better on paper but it's not a fair comparison — different chunking strategy inflates URL-level scores. P4 is the only pipeline that guarantees the right text reaches the LLM. We already updated TOP_K=10 in rag.py. Next step: replace the Gemini-only pipeline with P4 in production — runs fully locally, only the LLM call costs money.
